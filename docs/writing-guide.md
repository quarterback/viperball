# VIPERBALL HOUSE STYLE GUIDE
## Writing, Voice, and Coverage Standards — Version 2.0

---

## The premise

The target register is an NFL recap bullet. It assumes you watched. It names the play that turned the game and says why. It judges freely — "horrible run," "foolish decision," "absolute gift." It doesn't explain the sport and it doesn't perform confidence. It just moves.

Viperball coverage at its best reads exactly like that, with Viperball's scoring and functions in place of football's. Nothing else changes.

---

## Part 1: Write it like football.

It is football. Downs, drives, field position, touchdowns, field goals, run game, pass game. Use football's vocabulary throughout without comment.

The scoring has more channels than football. Snap kicks are worth five — write each attempt with distance and result, the way you'd write a field goal attempt, with the frequency of a three-pointer. Bells are half a point; final scores have halves. Write both as transactions, not events.

Interceptions carry a bonus possession. Write the sequence — the pick, then the possession that followed — because the sequence is what makes them worth more than a standard turnover.

Kill rate and power play efficiency are how the sport tracks lead management. Use them the way you'd use any team stat.

## Part 2: The verb is everything



### Running

| Action | Verb |
|---|---|
| Short yardage, power | **burrowed**, **churned**, **pounded** |
| Converted first down on the run | **found the line**, **turned the chains** |
| Breakaway | **burst**, **accelerated**, **was gone** |
| Between the tackles | **drove**, **fought**, **wrenched yards** |
| Sweep to the edge | **cut off the corner**, **turned the edge**, **got outside** |
| Option read | **took the pitch**, **kept it**, **made the read** |
| Dive play | **dove for**, **tucked and fell forward**, **fell into the end zone** |

Never write "ran for X yards" when a better verb exists. The yards tell themselves in the stat line. The verb tells the story.

### Kick passing

| Action | Verb |
|---|---|
| Short, quick kick to a receiver | **dropped**, **chipped**, **lofted** |
| Long kick downfield | **launched**, **sent over the top**, **found the channel** |
| Kick to a moving target | **led**, **hit in stride**, **threaded** |
| Exceptional kick placement | **placed it** |
| Receiver catches | **pulled it in**, **ran under**, **hauled it in**, **gathered** |
| Receiver drops | **couldn't hold**, **spilled it**, **put it down** |

The kick pass is not a pass in the forward-pass sense. It travels through air, not by hand. Write it with **aerial control verbs** — the kind used for chip shots in soccer or drop punts in AFL, not the kind used for spiral throws in football.

### Snap kicks

| Moment | Language |
|---|---|
| Lining up | "Reyes spots up from 41 yards" / "backs up, measures the angle" |
| The strike | "drops it, **strikes**" / "the snap kick is up" |
| Good | "**splits** the uprights" / "**finds** the posts" / "no doubt" / "right through the middle" |
| Just good | "**squeezes** through" / "just inside the right upright" |
| Missed | "**drifts** wide" / "**falls short**" / "shaves the post" |
| Big moment / big make | "**drills** it" / "**cold-blooded**" / "**unconscious**" |

A specialist who makes three snap kicks in a game is having a night. Write it like Steph Curry hitting three threes: each one builds on the last, and by the third the tension is in whether the defense will give her the look, not whether she'll convert.

### Tackles and defense

| Action | Verb |
|---|---|
| Clean stop in the backfield | **dropped**, **stuffed**, **met her behind the line** |
| Multiple defenders converge | **swarmed**, **closed her down** |
| Force backward | **drove her back**, **stood her up** |
| Open-field stop | **ran her down**, **chased down** |
| Forced turnover (lateral) | **stripped**, **jarred it free** |
| Bell recovery | **dove on**, **pounced on**, **covered up** |

## Part 3: Scoring moments — architecture

Every scoring play has a shape. The shape varies by the type of score, but the principle is the same: **build to the moment, name the moment, close with consequence**.

### The touchdown

Touchdowns are worth nine points. They end drives. They come from runs, kick passes, and occasionally lateral chaos. The narrative structure is:

1. **The approach** — where was she, what was the situation
2. **The score** — one decisive verb, first down to final foot
3. **The consequence** — score, momentum, what it means to the game

> *Rigsby had put twelve plays behind her getting to this point. One more kick, this one short and flat to the right flat, and Wright found the crease, drove for the pylon, and dove. Nine points. Rutgers 23, Mississippi State 14 — and all the pressure moved to Lapointe.*

Write touchdowns like rugby union match reporters write tries: trace the chain, earn the score, note its weight.

### The snap kick

The snap kick warrants its own ceremony. It's the premium score — five points from anywhere on the field. Build the ritual:

1. **Distance and angle** — "47 yards, straight in front"
2. **The motion** — drop, bounce, strike, trajectory
3. **The moment of truth** — often silent for a beat in broadcast prose
4. **Result** — definitive, numerical, consequential

> *Reyes backed off the hash, set herself. Forty-four yards. The snap kick climbed straight and high — split the posts dead center. Mississippi State 15, Rutgers 5. She looked exactly like she always looks: like she expected it.*

Note that Viperball snap kick specialists are **named every time they kick**. They're the equivalent of the free-taker in hurling — their personal accuracy record is part of the game's story.

### The field goal

Field goals are routine scoring sequences: a manufactured drive that ends in a set-piece kick. Three points. Write them quickly unless they're decisive:

> *The drive stalled at the 15. Reyes was summoned. Three points. Mississippi State 18, Rutgers 14.*

If the field goal is the winning score, expand it. If it's the third quarter and the drive was unremarkable, don't.

### The bell (½ point)

Write it as opportunism rewarded — the player who was alert when everyone else was watching the ball:

> *The lateral came loose and Castillo covered it in a flash. Bell for Rutgers. Her lead was now one and a half.*

## Part 4: Momentum and field position

### The spatial language

Viperball is a territorial sport. The field has two armies constantly repositioning, and good match writing reflects that spatial reality.

- **Own territory** / **their own half** — possession starting deep
- **The red zone** — inside the opponent's 20, snap kick range, maximum pressure
- **Snap kick country** — any position past midfield from which a specialist might attempt
- **Pinned back** — starting a drive at or behind your own 20 after a penalty, good punt, or Delta adjustment
- **Working room** — adequate field position, not great but functional
- **In the shadow of their own posts** — dangerous territory, opponent has everything to play for

When a team scores and gets pushed back on field position, write the field position:

> *Rutgers crossed again — their third touchdown — but they were back at their own 28 before the next possession. Mississippi State was already getting the ball on their own 35.*

### Momentum phrases native to Viperball

These should feel earned, not pasted in. Use them when the game actually shifts:

- **Wrestled it back** — a team retakes momentum after a score run
- **Opened up** — the drive broke through and yards came easily
- **Stalled** — a promising drive ran out of downs
- **Kept it alive** — a crucial 5th-down conversion that extended a drive
- **Absorbed the deficit** — a team responded to a score without panic
- **Put it away** — the final drive that ran the clock, or a score that made it unreachable
- **Running out the clock / taking a knee** — write it with finality

### Scoring runs

AFL and GAA journalism treats scoring runs — three points in a row, four of the next five — as momentum units. Viperball should too:

> *Mississippi State reeled off fifteen points in the second quarter without Rutgers answering. Three drives, three scores. Rutgers had gone from a manageable deficit to a hole.*

## Part 5: Drive narration

The drive is the unit. Not the play, not the quarter — the drive. Follow the ball through the possession: where it started, what moved it, what finished it or stopped it.

Rochester-Union: "Rochester's best drive of the day came in the fourth quarter. The possession featured a big play to Kane up the right side, a 4th-down conversion to the tight end, and a 22-yard catch to reach the Union 5." That's a drive. You know where they went and how they got there. You don't need every play.

For Viperball the same principle holds. Pick the plays that explain the drive's shape — the conversion that kept it alive, the kick that opened space, the stop on fifth down that ended it — and drop the rest.

> *Rutgers started at their own 22 after the kickoff. Castillo ran it three times for 18 yards combined, Rigsby found O'Brien on a short kick for the first down, and then went back to Castillo twice more to reach the 47. On fifth down, Rigsby kept it herself for nine yards. On sixth, she hit O'Brien at the back of the end zone. Rutgers 27, Mississippi State 20.*

That's six plays collapsed into four sentences. The drive has a shape: establish the run, use the kick pass to move the chains, Rigsby takes over when the game needs it. The reader follows it without needing a play-by-play.

When a drive fails, write the failure: where it stalled, what down it ended on, what the defense did to stop it.

> *Mississippi State went three-and-out on the next possession. Lapointe had a completion on first but the Rutgers line got to her on second and she threw it away, and a screen on third went for two yards. They punted from their own 31.*

Stats go at the end, after the game has been told through its drives.

## Part 6: Position mythology

Each position group has its own symbolic register. Write positions the way each sport's journalism has developed distinct archetypes for its key roles.

### The Zeroback

**She orchestrates. She conducts. She reads the field.**

The Zeroback gets cognitive verbs — **read**, **decided**, **recognized**, **exploited**. She makes the offense run. When she's hot, the offense flows. When she's not, blame is distributed tactfully.

Her kick-pass efficiency (completion percentage, yards, touchdowns) belongs in match reports the way passing yards belong in football coverage. But write it with AFL-style context: *Rigsby 13-from-30, 301 yards, three scores* — not as a stat block, as a sentence.

### The Viper

**She's the disruption principle.**

The Viper's alignment freedom means she can appear anywhere. She should be described with spatial specificity — not "O'Brien scored" but "O'Brien had drifted wide left before the snap, and when the kick came she was already three steps ahead of her corner." Her jersey color should be mentioned at least once per match report to remind the reader she's the axis of unpredictability.

Write the Viper's statistics (total yards, touchdowns, kick-pass receptions) the way AFL writers describe a key small forward: numerically specific, with attention to what her movements created for others.

### The Flankers

**They grind. They set the table. They earn it.**

Flankers get labor verbs — **churned**, **drove**, **burrowed**, **wrenched**. A strong Flanker performance is described through accumulation: seven carries, 113 yards, none of them easy. Write the difficulty into the yardage. "Castillo carried the ball seven times for 113 yards, and not one of them came without contact."

Halfbacks and wingbacks are distinguished in match reports by their function in the drive — a halfback taking dives between tackles is different from a wingback turning the edge. Name the type when it matters.

### The kicking specialist

Snap kick specialists are celebrities in this sport. They get named, their distances are noted, their makes are counted across the game. Write their nights the way boxing writers write a fighter working the body: each blow connecting, the cumulative effect building, and then the moment when the opponent simply can't answer.

> *Reyes had been here all night. Two snap kicks in the first half, one more to open the third. When she backed up from 44 yards with nine minutes left, nobody in the stadium doubted what was coming.*

## Part 7: The match report structure

Viperball match reports should follow this architecture:

### Opening (1–3 sentences)

Start with what the game was actually about. Sometimes that's the game. Sometimes it isn't.

NYU beat Wisconsin-La Crosse 72–47 and the recap leads with the win streak — 91 straight, second-longest in NCAA history — because that's the story. The score is in the second sentence. The game was never in doubt and the piece doesn't pretend otherwise.

For blowouts, lead with what the blowout means or revealed. For close games, lead with the margin and what produced it. For record-setting performances, lead with the record. The score is always in the first two sentences but it isn't always the first sentence.

**Don't open with**: "Rutgers University defeated Mississippi State 50–49 in a close contest."
**Open with**: "Aria Rigsby threw the ball thirty times and Rutgers won by a point."

### The turn

Name the moment the game changed and say why it changed it. One paragraph. For blowouts where there was no single turn, name the run that put it away.

### The drive narrative
Select three or four drives that define the game's shape. Not the ones with the most plays — the ones that explain the result. One from each team, the final drive of the winner.

### The performances
Two or three players, written in prose, not listed. Use box score data but write it as story: what they did, when it mattered, and what it looked like.

### The close
State the result plainly. Note what it means in the standings or the season. End on the team or player with the most to carry forward.

## Part 8: The score notation

Viperball scores should appear in the text as full numbers including halves:

> **Rutgers 50, Mississippi State 49**
> **Rutgers 50.5, Mississippi State 49** (if a bell made the half-point difference)

In running text during a drive, show the score after each scoring play — the way AFL match reports timestamp each goal with the running total. This gives the reader the scoreboard in their head without having to look it up.

Use the following formatting conventions:
- Touchdowns: **+9**
- Snap kick: **+5**
- Field goal: **+3**
- Bell: **+0.5**
- Safety: **+2**
- Pindown: **+1**

## Part 9: The register

AP wire is the floor. Athletic/Guardian is the ceiling. Everything in between is acceptable. Nothing below the floor is.

AP: transmits facts, assumes shared context, never explains the sport to the reader.
Athletic: same economy, adds judgment, trusts the reader to keep up.

The failure mode is neither of these. It's a piece that sounds like it's *trying to sound like* sports writing — reach sentences, performed compression, bows on paragraphs that already closed. When a sentence is announcing its own confidence, it isn't confident.

The fix is always to cut the sentence that's doing the announcing and let the one before it land.

"Three drop kicks, three good, all in hundred-degree heat." That's a sentence. "Ryan was unconscious out there, delivering when it mattered most." That's a sentence performing itself. Cut the second one every time.

## Part 10: The live box score as a working example

The box score from Rutgers vs. Mississippi State (Week 5) is a useful testing document. Here's how to read it for narrative:

**The story**: Rutgers outgained Mississippi State by 120 yards and dominated possession (68 plays to 37), but penalties killed them — 8 penalties for 95 yards, including three taunting calls on Mississippi State that still moved Rutgers into scoring position (perversely helping the visiting team). Rutgers won by a point.

**The performance**: Aria Rigsby (ZB50) had an ugly completion line — 13/30, 43% — but hit three touchdown kicks and managed the game from her own end zone for long stretches. Véronique Lapointe went 9/18 for Mississippi State, more efficient per attempt, but Mississippi State had six fewer possessions.

**The key plays**: The second-quarter lateral interception (Rutgers' own lateral picked off, giving Mississippi State a possession at the 71) stopped a Rutgers scoring drive and created a Mississippi State touchdown. Rutgers answered from a bonus possession that resulted from that same interception being their turnover — the Delta system at work. The Q3 snap kick from 44 yards to open the second half was a momentum marker for Mississippi State.

**What to lead with**: Not the final score. Lead with the 95 yards of penalties on a one-point game, and what it says about Rutgers that they won it despite themselves.

## Part 11: What the writing is actually about

This is the section the guide needed first.

Louisa Thomas doesn't cover Indiana football by describing what a quarterback does. She covers Cignetti's face on the sideline — the same half-scowl whether his team has just scored or been stuffed — and asks what that expression is for. Ray Ratto doesn't cover Bam Adebayo's 83-point game by explaining what free throws are. He asks why the performance will be forgotten, and why that's probably correct, and why we should think about that. Neither of them is inside the mechanics. Both of them are inside the meaning.

Viperball writing fails when it treats the sport as the subject. The sport is the setting. **The subject is always a person under pressure, or a team under pressure, or a coach who made a decision that will look right or wrong when we find out the score.**

### What this means in practice

**Lead with judgment, not description.**

Thomas opens on Cignetti's press conference answer — not to describe what he said, but because the answer reveals something about how he thinks. Ratto opens on the possibility that future people will deny Adebayo's game happened — not to describe the game, but because the denial tells us something true about how we treat records.

A Viperball match report's first sentence should contain a judgment:

> *Aria Rigsby threw the ball thirty times on the road and Rutgers won by a point. That's not a formula. That's a personality.*

> *Mississippi State played thirty-seven offensive plays and lost. Véronique Lapointe had a clean night — nine completions, no interceptions — and it wasn't enough. Sometimes efficiency isn't the story; volume is.*

> *Rutgers collected eight penalties for 95 yards in a one-point game. They won anyway. That fact says more about Mississippi State than it does about Rutgers.*

**The novelty of the sport is not the story.**

Every game has players who are doing something the writers find remarkable, and every game also has players who are doing something unremarkable that turns out to matter more. The trap in Viperball writing is to reach for the snap kick or the lateral chain because those are the visually strange elements. The discipline is to reach for what actually decided the game, and then describe that with as much precision as you can.

If the game turned on a 5th-down conversion, write about why that conversion was possible — the four plays that set it up, the defensive adjustment that opened the gap, the runner who had been wearing them down all afternoon. The down number is not the story. The down number is when the story resolved.

**The Zeroback is a coach who runs.**

Thomas's most useful observation about Cignetti is that everything he does is about controlling variables — the handshakes, the Chipotle order, the same face on every play. A Zeroback with 30 kick-pass attempts and a 43% completion rate is making thirty decisions in a game, each one under pressure, each one with a consequence. That's not a stat. That's a personality in action. Write it as one.

> *Rigsby went to O'Brien seven times in the first half and missed five of them. She went to her three times in the third quarter and two of them were touchdowns. That's not a slump and a hot streak. That's a conversation — Rigsby testing coverage, getting read, adjusting, then finding the answer she was looking for.*

**The defense has a story too.**

Alyson Phan had 16 tackles and three sacks. That's the most tackles and the most sacks in the game. She is not mentioned once in the narrative because she was on the losing side and the stats didn't translate to stops that changed the game. But you can write her night as a study in what doesn't work — the dominant individual performance trapped inside a structural failure. That's Ratto's move: the thing that should have mattered and didn't.

> *Phan was in Rigsby's face all afternoon — three sacks, relentless pressure, the kind of defensive performance that in most games produces a different outcome. But Rutgers' offense is built for exactly this: long drives, multiple downs, enough reps that a sack on third is recovered by a conversion on fourth. Phan put in the work. The architecture didn't reward it.*

**Coaches are people with theories about football.**

Thomas's piece works because she takes Cignetti's theory of football seriously — the repetition-as-mastery idea, the stable-expression-as-culture idea — and tests it against the results. Viperball has coaches with distinct offensive philosophies: East Coast, Chain Gang, Boot Raid. Those aren't aesthetic choices. They're theories about how football gets won. When an East Coast team faces a Chain Gang team, that's not just a stylistic mismatch — it's two theories of the game in competition. Write it as intellectual argument expressed in bodies.

**Let the box score be evidence, not summary.**

Ratto's NBA piece doesn't summarize what Adebayo did. It uses Adebayo's performance as evidence for a larger claim about memory and denial and how we process enormous things. The box score is your evidence. Use it to argue something.

The Rutgers/Mississippi State box score argues something clear: penalties cost Rutgers the dominant performance its yardage advantage deserved, but discipline alone doesn't win Viperball games, because the Delta system means a commanding lead creates its own drag. Rutgers was better. The game was close. Both of those facts are true, and the tension between them is the story.

**Find the one word and prove it.**

Anantharaman's Booker piece opens on "rude" — not a basketball term, not a stat, not a position description. A single abstract quality that names exactly what Booker's game does to the people trying to stop her. Then the piece earns it: the defender who stays in front of her, the midrange jumper that goes over anyway, the overwhelming pity. The concept arrives first. The evidence follows.

This is the move for player profiles and performance pieces. Don't describe what a player did. Find the word that names what her game does to opponents — or to her own team, or to the argument the coach was trying to make — and then prove it with specifics.

> *Castillo is exhausting. Not in the way a track sprinter is exhausting, but in the way a water leak is exhausting — always there, always present, always a few yards more than you thought she'd get. By the third quarter Mississippi State's linebackers had stopped trying to meet her in the backfield. They were waiting to see where she'd end up.*

The word doesn't have to be one word. It has to be one frame. Everything in the piece should be evidence for it.

- The novelty of a scoring system
- How a position works (the reader already knows)
- Whether Viperball is interesting (it is, that's why we're covering it)
- Whether women can compete at this level (they obviously can, that's why there's a league)

**What you're always writing about:**

- Whether this team is becoming what they think they are
- What this performance revealed about a player's character under pressure
- What the coach's decision in the fourth quarter tells us about their theory of football
- Whether this result was earned or stolen or neither

## Part 12: The arguments the sport has with itself

The Athletic's fourth-down analytics piece doesn't work because it explains what fourth down is. It works because it surfaces a debate the sport has been having for fifty years without knowing it — and shows that the "modern" answer was correct in 1969. The AFL umpiring piece works because "reasonable time" turns out to be a philosophical position, not a technical definition. The best Viperball writing generates the same quality: it finds the argument embedded in a game or a season and surfaces it as the sport thinking aloud.

Every mature sport has a set of ongoing arguments — tactical, philosophical, historical — that its coverage circles back to season after season. Viperball needs its own. Here are the ones already built into the sport's structure:

---

### The Delta Yards argument

The permanent debate: **Is the Delta system making the game better or just making leads harder to hold?**

This is Viperball's version of the fourth-down analytics argument. The Delta system penalizes scoring by degrading field position for the leading team. The argument in favor is that it creates lead management as a discipline — coaches who understand it play a different, more interesting game. The argument against is that it rewards defensive football and punishes dominance. Both are correct. Neither has been settled.

When a team wins a close game having been the better team all afternoon — like Rutgers against Mississippi State — the Delta argument is always present. Rutgers had 120 more yards. They won by one. The Delta system kept Mississippi State in a game they probably should have lost by two possessions. That's either the system working as designed or the system producing a false result, depending on your theory of what football is for.

**Write the Delta argument into game coverage whenever the margin is tighter than the stats suggest.** Don't explain the system. Surface the argument.

> *Rutgers gained 120 more yards and won by a point. Cignetti has a word for this in football: managed. Viperball coaches call it something else.*

---

### The kick-pass completion argument

**What does completion percentage actually measure in a sport where the incomplete kick is often a designed outcome?**

Rigsby went 13-for-30 against Mississippi State. That looks bad. But seven of those incompletions extended drives or drew penalties. The kick pass in Viperball is more like a contested mark attempt in AFL than a forward pass in football — the incomplete isn't always the failure. A kick that draws a horse-collar penalty is a 15-yard gain disguised as a miss. A kick that pulls the defense wide and opens the run game has done its job whether it's caught or not.

This argument should appear in coverage whenever a Zeroback's efficiency line looks worse than the result suggests — or better.

---

### The snap kick rate argument

**Are teams attempting snap kicks at the right frequency, or is the five-point value distorting decision-making?**

The Athletic's field goal argument translates directly: the book says go for it at this distance, don't go for it at that distance, and the psychology of the opposing coach changes based on your choice. Snap kicks are worth five points. The decision of when to attempt one versus when to keep driving for nine is the game's central coaching argument. A specialist who attempts four snap kicks in a game is either maximizing value or removing drives from the offense. Both framings are defensible.

**Write snap kick decisions as coaching arguments, not just outcomes.** The made kick is a fact. The decision to attempt it is a choice with a theory behind it.

---

### The lateral question

**Is the lateral chain an offense or a liability?**

It's never fully settled. Each lateral adds compounding fumble risk. The Chain Gang style builds offenses around lateral chains; the East Coast style treats them as last resort. After a lateral interception — like the one that turned the game in Q2 at Starkville — the argument restarts. Some coaches believe the lateral is structurally incompatible with lead management. Others believe the threat of the chain is worth the risk even if the chain itself is rarely executed.

When a lateral interception happens, don't just report it as a turnover. Note whose philosophy it represents and what it costs.

---

### The six-down argument

**Does the extra downs advantage favor the offense enough to compensate for the absence of the forward pass, or does it just make drives longer without making them more productive?**

This is the sport's foundational rules argument, still unresolved in the literature. Six downs means more fourth-down decisions, more fifth-down survival situations. Coaches who grew up in three-down or four-down football argue differently about risk. The law of diminishing possessions applies differently here than in four-down football — you can afford to be aggressive on fourth down because you still have two more. Or you can't, because you've already used your best plays. This is a real argument between coaches. Put it in coverage.

---

### How to write these arguments

These are not asides. They're not analytical boxes. They live inside the match report and the feature piece as **the thing the game is thinking about while the plays are happening**.

Thomas puts Cignetti's coaching philosophy inside the narrative of the game — his face, his Chipotle order, his list at the press conference are all expressions of the same theory. The analytics piece traces a decision back fifty years to show that the "new" thinking was available all along. The AFL piece finds a philosophy of the game baked into a word — "reasonable" — in a rule nobody reads.

Do that with Viperball. Find the argument in the play. Find the theory in the decision. ---

## Part 13: Coverage modes beyond the match report

A mature sport generates at least five distinct types of writing. Viperball has all of them available.

---

### The recap with opinions (the 3-2-1)

Fox Footy's 3-2-1 format is a useful template: three observations, two performances, one verdict. Each section carries a headline claim, quotes from commentators or coaches, and a number or sequence that anchors the argument. The register is broadcast-adjacent — written the way a confident color commentator talks.

The key moves: lead with a paradox or absurdity ("Carlton managed one goal in the second half and won"), use commentator quotes as evidence rather than decoration, and let numbers do rhetorical work without explanation.

For Viperball the equivalent: "Rigsby went 13-for-30 and threw three touchdown kicks. The completion percentage is a red herring. The touchdowns are the number."

Each section should contain a judgment the reader can argue with. Not "O'Brien had a good game" but "O'Brien is the reason Rutgers wins games they shouldn't."

---

### The character study

The Chad Kelly piece works because Fitz-Gerald never explains what the CFL is. Kelly's breakfast order, his uncle's shadow, the energy he has nowhere to put — these are the story. The CFL is the setting. Kelly's theory of himself is the subject.

Viperball character studies follow the same structure: find the person's theory of themselves, then test it against what the sport actually gave them.

A Zeroback who transferred from a four-down program and had to rebuild her entire decision-making instinct. A specialist kicker who played soccer until seventeen and still thinks about the ball with her feet rather than her hands. A coach whose system produces tight wins because she genuinely believes the Delta Yards penalty is an advantage, not a constraint, if you understand it correctly.

The sport appears as context. The person is the story. The game's specific vocabulary enters when it illuminates the person, not when it needs explaining.

---

### The analytics beat

The NFL Big Data Bowl piece and the skeletal tracking piece are both about the same thing: the gap between what the data captures and what the game actually contains. "If a metric just captures what happened, it's probably analytics. If it relies on a prediction or a probability of what could have happened, it's probably machine learning."

Viperball has real analytical tension built into its box scores. EPA (Expected Points Added), WPA (Win Probability Added), Viper Efficiency, mess rate — these are numbers that claim to measure something. The analytics beat asks what they miss.

What does EPA not capture about a kick pass that draws a penalty? What does Viper Efficiency miss about the Decoy Viper who creates yardage by being covered? What does mess rate tell a coach about lead management, and what does it conceal? What would a fractional tackle metric look like in a sport where six players might technically touch a ball carrier before she goes down?

Write the analytics beat as genuine inquiry. The numbers are trying to answer a question. The question is usually more interesting than the answer.

---

### The league beat (contracts, deployment, ambition)

The Daicos piece is pure league coverage: contract leverage, a player's frustration with how a coach used him, the expansion club money that changes the math, the salary cap argument. No game required.

Viperball has this territory. Conference alignment decisions. A Zeroback who wants to attempt more snap kicks than her coordinator will allow. A recruit whose college chose her for her kick-pass efficiency but whose natural game is built around runs. The CVL's expansion into new conferences and whether the talent pool is being diluted or deepened.

The league beat treats the sport as an industry and the players as professionals navigating it. Write it the way AFL and CFL writers write it: with respect for the complexity of the decisions and zero condescension toward the people making them.

## Part 14: Two formats in full

### The match report (the Limerick-Cork model)

The RTÉ match report has a structure worth naming precisely, because it's the most common output an LLM will be asked to produce from a box score.

**Opening paragraph**: Frame the game in its context before stating the result. Limerick-Cork is one of five meetings this year — the result matters less than what it reveals. "One would have excused either side of shadow boxing... but it was nothing but."

**Result sentence**: Buried in paragraph two, not paragraph one. Score, margin, character of the win.

**The halftime account**: One sentence. Level count, lead changes, wides, set pieces. Factual compression.

**Second half as a sequence**: Trace the momentum shift through named players and specific moments. Not "Limerick pulled away" but: Lynch intercepts a poor O'Mahony pass, plays through O'Neill, Collins has no chance. O'Connor's penalty with eight minutes left. Gillane winning the foul. The substitution that put O'Donoghue on the pitch for two minutes before he fouled Gillane. Every moment earns its name.

**The final paragraph**: Forward-looking. The result doesn't close the argument — it opens the next one.

**Full lineups with scorers**: Positions, subs, times, scoring breakdowns (frees, 65s, goals). This is the official record, not an afterthought.

Applied to Viperball — full match report register:

> Rutgers scored on their second possession and never trailed. Mississippi State cut it to one in the fourth quarter but couldn't get stops when they needed them.
>
> Rigsby hit O'Brien for 61 yards on the first drive, Castillo ran it in from the 4 on the next, and Rutgers led 18–5 before Mississippi State had found anything. Reyes made a snap kick from 44 to open the second half and the Lions were back within nine, but Rigsby answered on the next drive — found O'Brien twice for 40 yards combined, then kept it herself on sixth and scored. Rutgers 36, Mississippi State 24.
>
> Lapointe moved them down the field twice in the fourth, and a Reyes field goal made it 36–31 with six minutes left. Rutgers converted three straight fifth downs on the next drive and ran the clock out. Final: 50–49.
>
> Rigsby was 13-for-30 but hit three touchdown kicks and went 4-for-4 on fifth-down conversions when the game was on the line. Lapointe finished 9-for-18 with two touchdowns and no turnovers. Reyes made both snap kicks and the field goal. Mississippi State outgained Rutgers by 120 yards and lost by one.

### The Quick Slant format

For short-form game coverage, three sections: **What Happened**, **In the Spotlight**, **The Number**. Each one is two to four sentences. No transitions between them needed.

> **What Happened**: Rutgers led wire to wire but Mississippi State made it a one-point game in the fourth. Rigsby hit three touchdown kicks and Castillo ran for 74 yards on the ground. The Lions had more yards and fewer possessions and lost anyway.
>
> **In the Spotlight**: Reyes made both snap kicks and the field goal and had nothing to show for it. Mississippi State scored 45 points on offense and lost by one.
>
> **The Number**: 95 — Rutgers' penalty yards. They won by one.

**Lineups**: Then list full rosters with scoring breakdowns — touchdowns, snap kicks, field goals, bells — the way the GAA lists scorers. This is the official record. Every player who scored gets named. Every sub gets a time.

### The preview (the GAA podcast model)

The preview operates differently from the match report. It frames each game as a question with stakes. Not "here are the teams" but "here is what this game will decide and who decides it."

The GAA podcast does this through named tactical frames: "Moranball bounce," "minding Con O'Callaghan," "devoid of confidence." Each team gets a current state diagnosis and a specific challenge to answer. Division standings give every game a consequence beyond itself.

For Viperball weekly preview coverage:

Each game gets: the current state of both teams (one sentence each), the specific contest that will decide it (the Zeroback matchup, the kicking specialist advantage, the defensive stop rate), the stakes in the conference standings, and a verdict framed as a question rather than a prediction.

> **Rutgers (1-3) at Penn State (3-1) — Saturday, 1pm**
>
> Rutgers keeps finding ways to win games they're outplayed in. Penn State keeps finding ways to make opponents pay for the lateral. The question isn't whether Rigsby is better than Chen — she is. The question is whether Rutgers' defense can hold a team with the CVL's second-best conversion rate on 5th down, which is where Penn State lives and where Rutgers has been exposed twice. Penn State at home with a chance to go 4-1. Rutgers needing a win to stay relevant in the East. Penn State's coaches know how to manage a lead. Rutgers hasn't had to manage one yet.

## Part 15: Two more formats

### The multi-team preview (The Ringer model)

Anderson's playoff preview works through a consistent structure: team, question, evidence on both sides, verdict withheld. The question is always specific to that team's unresolved tension — not "can they win?" but "can Dante Moore do it alone?" or "which job is Kalen DeBoer actually coaching for?"

The form scales to any tournament or conference race. CVL playoff previews, conference tournament fields, top-25 rankings with a question attached to each entry. The discipline is: one question per team, the question must be answerable by a game result, and the question must be something the team's own season has raised rather than something imposed from outside.

For Viperball the equivalent questions live in the box score data and the season arc:

- **Can she do it in a road environment?** (A Zeroback with a split between home and road efficiency)
- **Is the defense actually good, or has the schedule been soft?** (A team's stop rate against run-heavy offenses versus kick-pass-heavy ones)
- **What happens when they have to play from behind their own 15?** (A team that's dominated games early and never had to manage a lead deep into the second half)
- **Is the kick-pass game real, or is it one Zeroback away from collapsing?** (Depth at the position, backup's efficiency)
- **Can they win the physical game?** (A team built on kick-pass efficiency meeting a Lockdown defense that forces the run)

Each question closes with two or three sentences of evidence — not a prediction, but the specific thing to watch for in the first game. Anderson's format never resolves its own questions. It puts the resolution in the reader's hands and gives them the frame to use when the game plays out.

One structural note: Anderson's questions escalate in specificity as they become harder to answer. The top seed gets "how do they deal with being hunted?" — broad, psychological. The underdog gets "who the hell are these guys?" — which is actually a specific argument about schedule strength and defensive grade. The question's specificity should be proportional to how much the reader already knows about the team.

### The business beat (SBJ model)

The SBJ analytics gap piece makes a specific argument: college football is a decade behind the NFL in data infrastructure, and the teams that close that gap will gain competitive advantage. The sport appears as an industry. The players appear as assets in a talent market. The argument has urgency because the landscape is changing (transfer portal, salary caps) faster than the tools are keeping up.

Viperball has equivalent business beat territory:

**The simulation as infrastructure.** The CVL runs on a simulation engine calibrated across thousands of games. That's a data infrastructure most real sports don't have at the college level. What does it tell coaches that box scores don't? What does it miss that box scores capture?

**Recruiting and the draft equivalent.** Which archetypes are being correctly valued? If Kicking Zerobacks are rare and the snap kick is worth five points on any down, is the market for them efficient? Are Speed Flankers overvalued because their highlight plays are visible and their fumble rate is buried in the play log?

**The analytics divide between CVL programs.** A school like Mississippi State running East Coast offense produces a comprehensible box score. A Chain Gang program produces a mess rate number that most coaches can't yet interpret. The programs that build analytical fluency around their own offensive systems first will have an edge. The ones that try to apply East Coast metrics to Chain Gang play will misread their own rosters.

**Conference alignment as a business decision.** Sixteen conferences, 187 teams, with lead management creating measurable field position advantages that home teams handle differently than road teams. Conference scheduling decisions are already data questions, whether or not CVL administrators treat them that way.

The SBJ beat writes the sport as a system of competitive pressures, not as a collection of games. It assumes the reader is a decision-maker — a coach, an administrator, a scout — and gives them something actionable. ---
